## Wien's displacement law

<KeyEquation
  id="wiens-law-smoke"
  title="Wien's Law (biography smoke fixture)"
  symbols={["T", "\\lambda_{peak}", "b"]}
>

$$
\lambda_{\text{peak}} = b \, T^{-1}
$$

Wien's displacement law relates the peak wavelength of a blackbody's
thermal emission to its temperature: hotter sources peak at shorter
wavelengths; cooler sources peak redder.

  <Observable>
    The wavelength at which a thermal source's emission spectrum
    reaches maximum intensity, as a function of the source's
    temperature.
  </Observable>

  <Assumption type="thermal-equilibrium">
    The source is in local thermodynamic equilibrium so the Planck
    distribution applies.
  </Assumption>

  <Assumption type="blackbody">
    The source emits as an ideal blackbody — no spectral lines, no
    continuum absorption shaping the peak.
  </Assumption>

  <Units symbol="T" unit="K" />

  <Units symbol="\\lambda_{peak}" unit="cm" />

  <BreaksWhen>
    Non-thermal emission (synchrotron, masers, line emission);
    optically-thin sources without thermal coupling; spectra
    dominated by absorption features rather than the continuum.
  </BreaksWhen>

  <CommonMisuse misconception="wiens-law-absorption-spectra">
    Applying Wien's law to identify the temperature of an
    absorption-line spectrum. The peak position depends on the
    continuum, not the absorption features — the law misidentifies
    the temperature when the spectrum is shaped by intervening
    cooler gas.
  </CommonMisuse>
</KeyEquation>
