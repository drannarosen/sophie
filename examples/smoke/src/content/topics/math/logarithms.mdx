<SkillReview.Card id="product-rule">
  <SkillReview.Prompt>
    What does $\log_b(xy)$ equal?
  </SkillReview.Prompt>
  <SkillReview.Answer>
    $\log_b(x) + \log_b(y)$ — logarithms turn products into sums.
    Falls out of the exponent product law: $b^{m+n} = b^m \cdot b^n$.
  </SkillReview.Answer>
</SkillReview.Card>

<SkillReview.Card id="power-rule">
  <SkillReview.Prompt>
    What does $\log_b(x^n)$ equal?
  </SkillReview.Prompt>
  <SkillReview.Answer>
    $n \cdot \log_b(x)$ — the exponent comes down as a multiplier.
    This is why magnitudes use the $-2.5 \log_{10}(F)$ convention:
    a factor-of-100 brightness change becomes a 5-magnitude
    difference because $\log_{10}(100) = 2$.
  </SkillReview.Answer>
</SkillReview.Card>

<SkillReview.Card id="change-of-base">
  <SkillReview.Prompt>
    Express $\log_2(x)$ using natural log ($\ln$).
  </SkillReview.Prompt>
  <SkillReview.Answer>
    $\log_2(x) = \ln(x) / \ln(2)$ — divide by $\ln$ of the new base.
    The general rule: $\log_a(x) = \log_b(x) / \log_b(a)$.
  </SkillReview.Answer>
</SkillReview.Card>
