import {
  Assumption,
  BreaksWhen,
  CommonMisuse,
  DerivationStep,
  Observable,
} from "@sophie/components";

<Observable>
  The wavelength at which a thermal source's emission spectrum peaks
  — i.e., where the continuum is brightest — as a function of the
  source's temperature.
</Observable>

<Assumption type="thermal-equilibrium">
  The source is in local thermodynamic equilibrium, so the Planck
  blackbody distribution governs its emission.
</Assumption>

<Assumption type="blackbody">
  The source emits as an ideal blackbody — no strong spectral lines
  or continuum absorption shaping the peak. Real stars approximate
  this; line-rich or non-thermal sources do not.
</Assumption>

<BreaksWhen>
  Non-thermal emission (synchrotron, masers, line-dominated spectra);
  optically-thin sources without thermal coupling; or spectra shaped
  primarily by absorption features rather than the continuum
  blackbody profile.
</BreaksWhen>

<CommonMisuse misconception="wiens-law-absorption-spectra">
  Applying Wien's law to an absorption-line spectrum to infer the
  source temperature. The peak position is set by the continuum, not
  the absorption features; using a line-dominated spectrum will
  misidentify the temperature when intervening cooler gas reshapes
  the apparent profile.
</CommonMisuse>

<DerivationStep label="Start from Planck's law">
  The Planck distribution for blackbody spectral radiance is
  $B_\lambda(T) = (2hc^2 / \lambda^5) \cdot 1 / (e^{hc/\lambda k_B T} - 1)$
  — the spectral energy density per unit wavelength at temperature
  $T$.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep label="Differentiate and set to zero">
  Take $\partial B_\lambda / \partial \lambda = 0$ to find the peak.
  The maximum sits at the wavelength where the spectral density's
  slope vanishes.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep label="Solve the transcendental equation">
  The resulting transcendental equation has root
  $hc / \lambda_{peak} k_B T \approx 4.965$, giving
  $b = hc / (4.965 \, k_B) \approx 0.29$ cm·K.
</DerivationStep>
