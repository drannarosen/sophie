import {
  Assumption,
  BreaksWhen,
  CommonMisuse,
  DerivationStep,
  Observable,
} from "@sophie/components";

<Observable>
  The total radiant power $L$ emitted by a thermal source of surface
  temperature $T$ and physical radius $R$, integrated over the full
  electromagnetic spectrum. We don't measure $L$ directly — it's
  inferred from flux + distance via the inverse-square law, with
  temperature and radius arriving from spectral fitting and
  interferometry respectively.
</Observable>

<Assumption type="thermal-equilibrium">
  The source is in local thermodynamic equilibrium, so the Planck
  blackbody distribution governs its emission and an effective
  temperature is well-defined.
</Assumption>

<Assumption type="blackbody">
  The source emits as an ideal blackbody — emissivity $\epsilon = 1$
  across all wavelengths. Real stars approximate this with effective
  temperatures slightly lower than what a perfect blackbody of the
  same total power would have.
</Assumption>

<Assumption type="spherical-source">
  The emitting surface is a sphere of well-defined radius $R$. Rapidly
  rotating, tidally distorted, or extended-atmosphere sources require
  a more careful surface-area treatment.
</Assumption>

<BreaksWhen>
  Non-thermal emission (synchrotron, pulsar magnetospheres, accretion
  jets); strong emission or absorption lines that dominate the
  bolometric output; sources where the photosphere is poorly defined
  (giants with extended atmospheres, supernova ejecta during the
  optically-thick phase).
</BreaksWhen>

<CommonMisuse misconception="brighter-equals-intrinsically-brighter">
  Treating apparent brightness as direct evidence of source radius.
  A small hot star and a large cool one can have identical
  luminosities; without independent temperature information from the
  spectrum, $R$ stays unconstrained. The Stefan-Boltzmann relation
  needs *two* of $\{L, R, T\}$ to fix the third.
</CommonMisuse>

<DerivationStep label="Start from the spectral radiance">
  The Planck distribution gives the power emitted per unit area per
  unit wavelength: $B_\lambda(T)$. Total intensity is the integral
  over all wavelengths: $\int_0^\infty B_\lambda(T) \, d\lambda$.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep label="Integrate Planck's law over wavelength">
  The closed-form result is
  $\int_0^\infty B_\lambda(T) \, d\lambda = (\sigma / \pi) \, T^4$,
  where $\sigma = 2\pi^5 k_B^4 / (15 h^3 c^2)$ collects the
  fundamental constants. This is the bolometric flux per unit
  emitting surface area, integrated over wavelength.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep label="Multiply by surface area and the per-steradian factor">
  A blackbody emits into the full $4\pi$ steradians; total radiant
  power is the surface flux times the surface area:
  $L = 4\pi R^2 \cdot \sigma T^4$. Three measurements — luminosity,
  radius, temperature — collapse into one relation; observers
  routinely use it both forward (predict L from R and T) and inverse
  (infer R when L and T are known).
</DerivationStep>
