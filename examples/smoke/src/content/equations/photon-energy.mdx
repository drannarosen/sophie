import {
  Assumption,
  BreaksWhen,
  CommonMisuse,
  DerivationStep,
  Observable,
} from "@sophie/components";

<Observable>
  The energy carried by a single quantum of electromagnetic radiation
  as a function of its wavelength or frequency. Direct measurement is
  impractical at optical wavelengths — detectors count photons and
  report their wavelength via diffraction or interference. Energy is
  inferred from the wavelength via $E = hc/\lambda$.
</Observable>

<Assumption type="vacuum-propagation">
  The photon propagates through vacuum, so the wavelength used in
  $E = hc/\lambda$ is the wavelength the photon would have without
  any intervening medium. Real instruments correct for
  index-of-refraction shifts in air or glass before applying the
  relation.
</Assumption>

<Assumption type="monochromatic">
  The photon has a well-defined single wavelength. Reasonable for a
  narrow atomic line; questionable for a broadband continuum, where
  the relation applies per-wavelength but cannot be summed
  meaningfully without integrating against the spectrum.
</Assumption>

<BreaksWhen>
  Strong gravitational potentials between source and observer shift
  the observed wavelength via gravitational redshift; using the lab
  $\lambda$ in $E = hc/\lambda$ then gives the energy in the source
  frame, not the observer frame. Cosmological redshift behaves the
  same way: a quasar's Ly$\alpha$ photons emitted at 1216 Å reach
  Earth at $1216(1+z)$ Å.
</BreaksWhen>

<CommonMisuse misconception="common-student-confusions">
  Treating frequency and wavelength as interchangeable variables in
  arithmetic. They have an inverse relationship: doubling $\lambda$
  halves $\nu$. A factor-of-two error in this step is common when
  translating between "the 5 GHz line" and "the 6 cm line" without
  first computing $c / \nu$.
</CommonMisuse>

<DerivationStep label="Planck's quantization hypothesis (1900)">
  Planck postulated that blackbody emitters exchange energy with the
  radiation field only in discrete packets $E = h\nu$, where $h$ is
  a new fundamental constant. This was a mathematical fix for the
  ultraviolet catastrophe; Planck himself initially viewed it as a
  bookkeeping trick rather than a physical claim.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep label="Einstein's photon (1905)">
  Einstein extended Planck's quantum from a property of emitters to a
  property of the light itself. The photoelectric effect — electrons
  ejected from metal only above a threshold frequency — required that
  light arrive in indivisible quanta of energy $h\nu$, not as a
  continuous wave.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep label="Convert frequency to wavelength">
  Use $c = \lambda \nu$ to rewrite $E = h\nu$ as $E = hc/\lambda$.
  The wavelength form is the convenient one for spectroscopy because
  detectors measure wavelength directly via diffraction. The shortcut
  $hc \approx 1240\,\text{eV nm}$ lets you read photon energy in eV
  off a wavelength in nm without further unit conversion.
</DerivationStep>
