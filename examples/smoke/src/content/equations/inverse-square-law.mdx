import {
  Assumption,
  BreaksWhen,
  CommonMisuse,
  DerivationStep,
  Observable,
} from "@sophie/components";

<Observable>
  The flux $F$ received from a source at distance $d$ — the energy
  per unit time per unit area arriving at our detector. We literally
  count photons; their rate per unit area is the observable.
</Observable>

<Assumption type="isotropic-emission">
  The source emits the same power in every direction. Beamed
  emitters (relativistic jets, pulsar lighthouses) violate this and
  require a directional beaming correction.
</Assumption>

<Assumption type="point-source">
  The source is small enough relative to the observation distance
  that geometric extension can be ignored. Resolved extended sources
  (nearby galaxies, large nebulae) require integrating flux over
  solid angle.
</Assumption>

<Assumption type="transparent-medium">
  No absorption or scattering between source and observer. Dust and
  gas along the line of sight produce extinction that dims observed
  flux beyond the geometric $1/d^2$ fall-off.
</Assumption>

<BreaksWhen>
  The source is beamed (relativistic jets), spatially resolved
  (extended galaxies, nearby nebulae), or seen through a dusty /
  absorbing medium. At cosmological distances the simple Euclidean
  $d^2$ term is replaced by the luminosity distance, which folds in
  redshift and time-dilation corrections.
</BreaksWhen>

<CommonMisuse misconception="brighter-equals-intrinsically-brighter">
  Treating apparent brightness as a direct proxy for intrinsic
  luminosity. Two equally luminous stars at very different distances
  can appear at wildly different fluxes; the brightness comparison
  says nothing about the sources until the distance term is fixed.
</CommonMisuse>

<DerivationStep label="Set up the emitting sphere">
  Imagine a source of total luminosity $L$ (energy per unit time)
  radiating uniformly in all directions. Consider an imaginary
  sphere of radius $d$ centred on the source.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep label="Conservation of energy on the sphere">
  Every photon emitted crosses this sphere exactly once. The total
  power passing through the sphere equals $L$; spread it over the
  sphere's surface area $4\pi d^2$.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep label="Divide power by area">
  Flux is power per unit area at the detector:
  $F = L / (4\pi d^2)$. Purely geometric — no dependence on
  wavelength or source physics beyond isotropy.
</DerivationStep>
