import {
  Assumption,
  BreaksWhen,
  CommonMisuse,
  DerivationStep,
  Observable,
} from "@sophie/components";

<Observable>
  The orbital speed $v$ of a tracer (a star, a gas parcel, a
  satellite galaxy) at distance $r$ from the gravitating
  centre — measured via Doppler shift of a known spectral line.
</Observable>

<Assumption type="circular-orbit">
  The orbit is circular. Elliptical orbits replace $v^2/r$ with the
  full vis-viva relation involving the semi-major axis.
</Assumption>

<Assumption type="newtonian-gravity">
  Speeds are non-relativistic ($v \ll c$) and the gravitational field
  is weak. Strong-field or relativistic regimes (near black holes,
  cosmological scales) require general relativity.
</Assumption>

<Assumption type="spherical-mass-distribution">
  The enclosed mass acts as if concentrated at the centre (Newton's
  shell theorem) — the mass distribution interior to $r$ is
  spherically symmetric and exterior mass exerts no net force.
</Assumption>

<BreaksWhen>
  The orbit is highly elliptical; the system is relativistic
  (near black holes, AGN central engines); the mass distribution is
  not spherically symmetric (galaxy bars, triaxial halos); or
  pressure support (in gas) competes with gravity. Galaxy rotation
  curves are read with this equation precisely *because* its
  breakdown — the predicted Keplerian fall-off failing to appear —
  is the signature of dark matter.
</BreaksWhen>

<CommonMisuse misconception="dark-matter-is-just-hidden-normal-matter">
  Reading "more mass than we see" as "more *baryonic* matter than
  we see." The equation gives total enclosed mass; nothing about it
  distinguishes luminous matter from anything else. Searches for
  faint baryonic candidates (MACHOs, brown dwarfs, cold gas) have
  *not* closed the gap; the missing mass is non-baryonic.
</CommonMisuse>

<DerivationStep label="Balance gravity against centripetal acceleration">
  For a tracer of mass $m$ on a circular orbit, Newton's second law
  with centripetal acceleration gives
  $m v^2 / r = G M m / r^2$ — gravitational attraction provides the
  centripetal force.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep label="Cancel the tracer mass">
  The tracer mass $m$ appears on both sides and cancels:
  $v^2 / r = G M / r^2$. The orbital speed depends only on the
  enclosed mass $M$ and radius $r$, not on the tracer's mass —
  Galileo's principle in disguise.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep label="Multiply through by $r$">
  Result: $v^2 = G M / r$. Rearranged for mass:
  $M = v^2 r / G$.
</DerivationStep>
