import {

  BreaksWhen,
  CommonMisuse,
  DerivationStep,
  Observable,
} from "@sophie/components";

<Observable>
  The discrete energies an electron may hold while bound to a hydrogen
  nucleus. Each integer $n \geq 1$ labels one of hydrogen's allowed
  orbits; transitions between them produce the absorption and emission
  lines visible in stellar spectra. We never measure $E_n$ directly —
  we infer it from the wavelength of the photon emitted or absorbed
  during a transition.
</Observable>

<Assumption type="single-electron">
  Exactly one electron orbits a single proton. The formula extends to
  hydrogen-like ions (He$^+$, Li$^{2+}$, ...) with a $Z^2$ scaling but
  breaks for any atom with two or more electrons, because
  electron–electron interactions reshape the level structure.
</Assumption>

<Assumption type="point-nucleus">
  The proton is a structureless point charge. Finite nuclear size,
  spin–orbit coupling, and QED corrections shift levels by parts in
  $10^5$ — important for precision spectroscopy, irrelevant for
  classifying stellar lines.
</Assumption>

<Assumption type="non-relativistic">
  The electron's orbital velocity is small compared to $c$. Failure of
  this assumption produces the fine-structure splitting visible in
  high-resolution spectra: H$\alpha$ splits into multiple sub-lines
  separated by $\sim 0.36$ cm$^{-1}$.
</Assumption>

<BreaksWhen>
  Multi-electron atoms — helium, iron, calcium, every atom heavier
  than hydrogen — have level structures fundamentally different from
  the $13.6\,\text{eV}/n^2$ pattern. Strong external magnetic fields
  (Zeeman effect) or electric fields (Stark effect) split each
  $n$-level into sub-states. Highly excited Rydberg states
  ($n \gtrsim 100$) ionize easily from ambient radiation and don't
  behave as isolated bound states.
</BreaksWhen>

<CommonMisuse misconception="common-student-confusions">
  Applying $13.6\,\text{eV}/n^2$ to a non-hydrogen atom. Every atom
  stellar atmospheres care about beyond hydrogen has its own level
  diagram. The Bohr formula is specifically hydrogenic; using it on
  the Ca II K line at 3934 Å gives the wrong answer by orders of
  magnitude.
</CommonMisuse>

<DerivationStep label="Quantize angular momentum">
  Bohr postulated that bound-electron angular momentum is quantized in
  units of $\hbar$: $L = n\hbar$ for integer $n \geq 1$. This move
  turns a continuous classical orbit problem into a discrete spectrum
  of allowed states.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep label="Balance Coulomb and centripetal forces">
  Set the Coulomb attraction $ke^2/r^2$ equal to the centripetal
  force $m_e v^2 / r$, then combine with $m_e v r = n\hbar$ to solve
  for the orbit radius $r_n = n^2 a_0$, where
  $a_0 = \hbar^2 / (m_e k e^2) \approx 5.29 \times 10^{-9}$ cm is the
  Bohr radius.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep label="Compute total energy at each radius">
  Add kinetic and potential energy at $r_n$:
  $E_n = -k e^2 / (2 r_n) = -13.6\,\text{eV} / n^2$. The negative
  sign signals binding; $E_n \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$ marks the
  ionization threshold.
</DerivationStep>
