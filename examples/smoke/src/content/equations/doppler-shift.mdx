import {
  Assumption,
  BreaksWhen,
  CommonMisuse,
  DerivationStep,
  Observable,
} from "@sophie/components";

<Observable>
  The fractional shift of a known spectral line's wavelength from its
  laboratory rest value, observed in the spectrum of a moving source.
  Comparing the observed wavelength of a line we can identify (Ca II
  K, H$\alpha$, Na D) against its lab value yields the source's
  line-of-sight velocity. Sign convention: $\Delta\lambda > 0$ for
  redshift (recession), $\Delta\lambda < 0$ for blueshift (approach).
</Observable>

<Assumption type="non-relativistic">
  The radial velocity is small compared to $c$: $|v_r| \ll c$. The
  expression is the first-order term in a Taylor expansion of the
  full relativistic Doppler formula. At $v_r = 0.1c$ the
  approximation errs by $\sim 5\%$; at $v_r = 0.5c$ it errs by
  $\sim 30\%$.
</Assumption>

<Assumption type="radial-only">
  The formula gives the line-of-sight component of velocity. Motion
  perpendicular to the line of sight (proper motion) shifts the
  observed wavelength only at second order $(v_t/c)^2$ and is
  unobservable from a single spectrum.
</Assumption>

<Assumption type="rest-frame-known">
  The line's rest-frame wavelength $\lambda_0$ is known from
  laboratory spectroscopy or atomic theory. Without an independently
  identified line, you cannot disentangle a Doppler shift from a
  source emitting at an unknown wavelength.
</Assumption>

<BreaksWhen>
  Relativistic velocities ($|v_r| \gtrsim 0.1c$) require the full
  relativistic Doppler formula
  $\lambda_{\text{obs}}/\lambda_0 = \sqrt{(1+\beta)/(1-\beta)}$
  with $\beta = v_r/c$. Strong gravitational potentials add a
  gravitational redshift on top of the kinematic shift. Hot or
  turbulent gas broadens lines via thermal Doppler and turbulent
  broadening, mimicking a shift if the line profile is asymmetric.
</BreaksWhen>

<CommonMisuse misconception="common-student-confusions">
  Using the formula to measure proper motion (sideways motion). Doppler
  shift is sensitive only to the line-of-sight component; a star
  moving perpendicular to the line of sight at 1000 km s$^{-1}$
  shows $\Delta\lambda = 0$ in its spectrum. Proper motion requires
  astrometric measurement of the star's position over time, not
  spectroscopy.
</CommonMisuse>

<DerivationStep label="Geometric wave-emission setup">
  A source emits wavefronts at rest-frame period $\tau_0 =
  \lambda_0/c$. While one wavefront travels toward the observer, the
  source moves a distance $v_r \tau_0$ along the line of sight,
  changing the spacing of successive wavefronts arriving at the
  detector.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep label="Compute the observed wavelength">
  The observed spacing between wavefronts becomes
  $\lambda_{\text{obs}} = \lambda_0 + v_r \tau_0
   = \lambda_0 (1 + v_r/c)$. Rearranging:
  $\Delta\lambda / \lambda_0 = v_r/c$. Positive $v_r$ (recession)
  stretches the wavelength; negative $v_r$ (approach) compresses it.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep label="Relate to the relativistic limit">
  The exact relativistic result is
  $\lambda_{\text{obs}}/\lambda_0 = \sqrt{(1+\beta)/(1-\beta)}
   \approx 1 + \beta + \tfrac{1}{2}\beta^2 + \mathcal{O}(\beta^3)$.
  The non-relativistic formula keeps only the linear term. Use the
  full form when $|\beta| \gtrsim 0.1$ or when the second-order
  transverse Doppler matters (precision spectroscopy of high-redshift
  sources).
</DerivationStep>
