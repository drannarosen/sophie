# Funding roadmap

How Sophie gets funded over the next 5 years, why this shape and not
others, and how every funding decision serves the pre-tenure case
rather than competing with it.

## The four-path comparison

Four plausible long-term shapes for Sophie. Each has a distinct money
source, a distinct accountability structure, and distinct
compatibility with AGPL and pre-tenure status.

| Path | Money source | AGPL-compatible? | Pre-tenure-compatible? |
|------|--------------|-------------------|--------------------------|
| **1. Funded OSS infrastructure** | NSF, NASA, foundations | Yes | **Yes — preferred** |
| **2. Indie SaaS / dual-licensed** | Hosted version, premium AI, commercial license | Yes (via dual-license) | No — defer to post-tenure |
| **3. VC-scale company** | Venture capital | No (forces rewrite or aggressive dual-license) | **Off the table** |
| **4. Academic strategic asset** | Anna's salary + release time | Yes | **Yes — preferred** |

### Path 1 — funded open-source infrastructure

Grant funding underwrites Sophie's development. NSF, NASA, and
private foundations all fund nascent OSS infra for research and
education. Sophie remains AGPL; commercial activity is deferred or
declined; the platform serves educators broadly.

Pro: AGPL is fine. NSF/NASA funding directly strengthens the tenure
case. Pays for RA/postdoc labor that accelerates the platform.

Con: 20–30% of work-time becomes proposal writing. Funding gaps are
real. No equity upside if Sophie becomes a phenomenon.

### Path 2 — indie SaaS / dual-licensed

Sophie remains AGPL OSS for self-hosters, with a hosted product
("Sophie Cloud") and a commercial license for users who cannot adopt
AGPL. Revenue from hosted + premium AI features + institutional
licensing supports continued development.

Realistic 5-year ceiling executed seriously: $200K–$2M ARR. Lifestyle
business shape, not a venture outcome.

Pro: Anna owns it. Independence post-tenure. Revenue compatible with
AGPL via dual-licensing (provided
[CLA infrastructure](agpl-rationale.md#the-cla-prerequisite) is in
place).

Con: Running a business — sales, support, churn, taxes, payroll. SDSU
Conflict of Interest / Conflict of Commitment policies become
load-bearing. **Pre-tenure, this is incompatible with the day job.**

### Path 3 — VC-scale company

Sophie becomes EdTech-Inc., raises venture funding, sells enterprise
contracts to universities and publishers. Anna takes leave from SDSU
or leaves academia.

Realistic assessment: probably not the right shape. EdTech VC is a
graveyard — Top Hat raised $130M+ and reportedly struggles; Cengage's
MindTap is a slow decline; Coursera and 2U are public-company
basket-cases. Sophie's TAM is narrow. AGPL is structurally
incompatible with venture economics. Faculty rarely make this jump
without leaving academia (Luis von Ahn → Duolingo is the famous
exception, not the rule).

**Decision: off the table** unless conditions change dramatically
(co-founder, exit from academia, etc.).

### Path 4 — academic strategic asset

Sophie underwrites the research program, anchors the tenure case, and
makes Anna a leading voice in "AI-augmented STEM authoring." The
platform produces papers and grants; the platform itself is the
deliverable; commercialization is secondary or never.

Pro: Lowest risk. Compatible with everything else. AGPL is fine.
Sophie becomes evidence in the tenure file.

Con: No money beyond salary. Sophie may stagnate if Anna can't get
release time. May feel anticlimactic if the platform is genuinely
good.

## The recommended spine: Path 1 + Path 4 hybrid

For Anna's pre-tenure situation (year 3, just reappointed, ~2–3 years
to tenure decision), the dominant strategy is **Path 1 + Path 4
together**, with Path 2 deferred and Path 3 declined.

**Why this combination:**

- **Path 4 sets the tenure-case foundation.** Sophie generates
  publishable papers and supports teaching evaluations. Every Sophie
  hour does double-duty toward the tenure file.
- **Path 1 funds Path 4.** NSF/NASA grants pay for the work that
  produces the papers. External funding is itself a tenure-case asset
  regardless of whether DBER counts in the dept.
- **Path 2 is preserved as a year-3+/post-tenure option.** AGPL +
  CLA infrastructure keeps dual-licensing viable. Decision deferred
  to 2028+.
- **Path 3 is declined.** Incompatible with pre-tenure status and
  with AGPL; EdTech VC outcomes don't justify the cost.

See [Grants](grants/index.md) for the specific programs in play and
[Papers](papers/index.md) for the publication pipeline that
underwrites the tenure case.

## Why pre-tenure shapes everything

The single most important constraint:

> **Sophie hours should ideally do double-duty** — generate a
> publication, generate funding, or generate measurable
> teaching-effectiveness evidence. Hours that don't do double-duty
> are expensive.

Operational consequences:

- **Every proposal counts** — even one that doesn't get funded
  demonstrates productivity and clarifies the research narrative.
- **Every Sophie feature should be either** (a) load-bearing for a
  course Anna teaches, (b) the subject of a forthcoming paper,
  (c) required by an active proposal, or (d) demanded by the
  platform's core contract. Features outside these four categories
  are deferrable.
- **Speaking and workshops count as service**, which counts toward
  tenure. AAS-EPD, AAPT, PER conferences — start now (see
  [Commercialization → speaking](commercialization.md#post-tenure-revenue-streams)).
- **DBER positioning is the single highest-leverage open question.**
  If SDSU astronomy counts education research, Sophie is the
  tenure-case centerpiece. If not, Sophie has to coexist with a
  parallel traditional-astro publication track. See
  [DBER positioning](dber-positioning.md).
